The shift

Green metrics. Red outcomes.

Service levels are met. Uptime is high, tickets close in SLA, the dashboard is green. And the business outcome still doesn't move. The gap is not whether the system works. It is whether the experience of using it moves the number the business is accountable for.

  • The SLA layer measures the system. Availability, response time, resolution. Necessary, and not sufficient.
  • The outcome layer measures the business. Retention, cost-to-serve, regulatory standing, productivity.
  • Between them sits experience. The thing that decides whether a green SLA becomes a moved outcome, or just a green SLA.

What sets the work apart

Finding the metric that moves.

Out of hundreds of things you could measure, one behavioral signal actually moves the outcome. Finding it is the craft, and it is what Launch does. Launch reads it from how people behave, then proves it in the data they already generate.

  • Qualitative. The behavioral tell. What people do when the experience fails them, observed in the real environment.
  • Quantitative. The proof. The data that shows this signal, and not the other ninety-nine, is the one wired to the outcome.
  • The result. A metric that converts to business value and cannot be gamed. The discipline that separates a designed experience from a surveyed one.

The discipline

A metric that matters vs. a vanity score.

The trap the category falls into: building the experience layer out of scores that measure themselves. A rising composite index feels rigorous. It terminates in itself. The measures that earn their place are the ones wired to a business outcome.

× The vanity metric

→ The metric that matters

It converts to money and it cannot be gamed. That is the difference between a designed experience and a measured one.
