The argument to be ready for

A metric is not a KPI. The difference is mathematical.

You will hear it: "some metrics just don't connect to outcomes." The answer is not to disagree. It's to reframe. If you're tracking a metric that doesn't connect to an outcome, you're tracking the wrong metric. The disconnect they're describing is real, and it's the evidence the KPI hasn't been found yet. That's the work.

The test: if a metric at 100% still doesn't deliver the business outcome, it is not the KPI. It's contributory.
IVR: authentication vs. containment
ContributoryAuthentication rate, intent coverage. At 100% each, still no guaranteed cost reduction.

Employee sentiment vs. attrition
ContributorySentiment. At 100% happy, still no guaranteed outcome. People can love the job and underperform, or hate it and deliver.
↓ one input among many to
KPIAttrition. Laddered from sentiment, absence, and more.
↓ which moves
OutcomeContinuity, ROI, release timing.

The science is knowing a metric from a KPI. The art is finding the KPI, because the one that moves is usually not the one being tracked. That takes someone skilled at reading the relationship between numbers and behavior. The same applies to uptime and personalization: contributory, never the whole outcome on their own.

The trap, and why we don't fall in it

A vanity metric vs. a metric that matters.

Here's the mistake the whole category makes, and the one our competitors will make on this exact kind of deal. It's easy to build an experience score that measures itself. It's hard to find the number wired to a dollar. We know the difference because we look for it on purpose.

× The vanity metric
A rising "Experience Score" (EIS)
A 0–100 composite that rolls sentiment and sub-scores into one index. The roof becomes “the score went up.” Feels rigorous: dashboards, weightings, trends, but it terminates in itself.
Why it fails the test: a higher score isn't a business outcome. It's the sentiment tier in a new costume, measuring experience against itself, not against the dollar the buyer must defend. “Confidence 63 → target 80” is a feeling with a target painted on.
→ The metric that matters
A behavioral count wired to the dollar
The one thing people do when the experience fails, already counted, proven in the data to move the outcome the buyer is accountable for.
Device not ready Day 1  →  108 new hires × 2.1-day wait  →  $62K productivity loss  →  attrition the bank is trying to stop
Why it wins: it converts to money and it can't be gamed. The score can drift green while this stays red, which is exactly the gap that costs the client.

Everyone can build the score. Finding the metric that matters is the discipline, and the moat.

The Experience Score isn't a strawman. It's the most common pattern in the category, including in our own strongest work. Naming the trap is how we make sure we engineer past it on every engagement.

The engagement is sized by the shape of the work.

Not a calendar. Six variables set the scope of Discovery, and they are knowable per pursuit.

1Persona type. Cliche and easy to reach (a Whole Foods shopper) or niche and hard (a field tech in oil and gas). The second costs more to access.
2Subjects per persona. Five is the working floor for a defensible read.
3Systems in the experience. How many connect across the workflow/experience being audited.
4Personas per system. How many different people touch the same experience.
5Observable means observed. Digital can be analyzed remotely. Physical work requires on-site tag-alongs, which cost more.
6The 1:3 rule. Every hour with a subject carries three more in prep and synthesis.

Who does this work

Two senior profiles. The signal lives in the loop between them.

Discovery pairs two senior profiles. The signal is found in the loop between them.

Qualitative

Senior Design Researcher

Reads the behavioral tell in the real environment and frames which metric is a candidate to be the one that moves. Keeps the work design-led.

Activities: discovery strategy, discovery execution, on-site observation, synthesis, the insights report. The scarcer, more senior hire.
Quantitative

Senior Data Scientist

Proves the signal is wired to the outcome and sizes it. Causal inference, not predictive model-building.

Activities: funnel and cohort analysis, lagged correlation and lead-lag testing, regression and time-series, quasi-experiments, sizing the lever. A dedicated ML engineer enters only when data scale or transcript and telemetry volume demands it.

The researcher surfaces the candidate tells. The data scientist tests which one is wired to the outcome. The art is the loop between them, with the Senior Researcher holding the synthesis that keeps it from terminating in a vanity score.

The Design phase shifts shape: one designer per experience, about two weeks each. Concept-and-validation adds a sprint per cycle, up to three. Discovery is front-loaded and senior; design and measurement run leaner.
